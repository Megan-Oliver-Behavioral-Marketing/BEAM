Guide

How to run a BEAM diagnosis.

BEAM produces a working hypothesis — not a certainty. The goal is to identify the condition most worth testing before designing an intervention.

Steps

Six steps from naming the missing behavior to designing the test.

  1. Name the action
  2. Gather evidence
  3. Name the barrier
  4. Read the emotional state
  5. Examine the moment
  6. Match the intervention
  7. Design the test

BEAM is a diagnostic framework — not a prescription. Complete each step using observed behavior wherever possible. If a conclusion is based on instinct, name it as an assumption and test it before treating it as fact.

Before you start

Most conversion problems get treated as messaging problems. Better copy gets written. A new creative gets tested. The behavior does not change. BEAM exists because the barrier is usually not the message.

Before running a diagnosis, accept one working premise: if someone is not acting, something specific is blocking them. Your job is to find that thing — not to produce a more convincing argument for action.

1
Define the action

Name the specific behavior that is not happening.

Not awareness. Not engagement. Not interest. The exact observable action — submit the form, book the appointment, complete checkout, attend the event, move from one page to the next.

If you cannot name it specifically, start there. Vague problems produce vague diagnoses and untestable interventions.

Ask: What would it look like if this worked? What is the single action I need this person to take?

2
Gather evidence

Document what you can actually observe.

Where does behavior break? Use analytics to locate the drop-off. Use recordings or usability testing to observe what happens at that point. Use customer language — feedback, reviews, support tickets — to understand why.

If evidence is incomplete, name your conclusions as assumptions. An assumption is not a problem. An assumption treated as a fact is.

Ask: What do I actually know versus what am I guessing? Where is the evidence weakest?

3
Name the barrier

Identify which condition is blocking the action.

BEAM identifies three primary barrier types. Ability — the person cannot understand, locate, evaluate, or complete the action. Environment — the path, timing, process, or surrounding conditions resist action. Motivation — the action does not feel relevant, worthwhile, or urgent right now.

Choose the strongest working diagnosis based on your evidence. Then ask: what else could explain this behavior? Challenge the first explanation before committing to it.

Ask: If the message were perfect, would the behavior still not happen? If yes, the barrier is not the message.

4
Read the emotional state

Identify what the person is feeling at the moment of decision.

Not how your audience feels in general — what emotional state is most likely present at the specific point where the action needs to happen. Confusion slows action. Overwhelm delays it. Anxiety avoids it. Confidence enables it.

Emotional state shapes how information is interpreted, how much risk feels tolerable, and whether the offer feels safe. The same message lands differently depending on what the person is feeling when they encounter it.

Ask: What is this person experiencing in the seconds before and during the decision? What evidence suggests that state?

5
Examine the moment

Document the conditions present at the point of decision.

What does the person see? What do they know? What remains uncertain? What effort is required? What feels at risk? What competes for their attention?

The decision moment is the totality of conditions surrounding the action — not just the message or the offer. The same person can behave differently when one of these conditions changes.

Ask: Does the moment support or resist the action I need to happen — and why?

6
Match the intervention

Choose the response that addresses the diagnosed condition.

Use clarification when the barrier is ability — confusion, overwhelm, or unclear next steps. Simplify the path. Reduce cognitive load. Make the action unmistakable.

Use elevation when the barrier is motivation — low urgency or weak relevance. Connect the action to an immediate priority. Create a reason to act today.

Use reinforcement when the emotional state is uncertainty or low confidence. Provide proof or reassurance at the moment it is needed.

Use connection when the barrier is skepticism or distrust. Build emotional safety before increasing the ask.

Ask: Does this intervention address the diagnosed barrier — or is it the tactic I already wanted to use?

7
Design the test

Write a hypothesis that can be proven wrong.

BEAM produces a working diagnosis — not a guarantee. The test determines whether the diagnosis was useful. A good hypothesis names the change, the expected behavior, and the reason.

If we [change this condition], then [this person] will be more likely to [take this action] because [the diagnosed barrier has been reduced].

Then identify the success metric — the specific, measurable behavior that would indicate the intervention worked. Run the test. Return to step one with what you learn.

The goal is not a convincing explanation. It is a sharper hypothesis and a test worth running.
