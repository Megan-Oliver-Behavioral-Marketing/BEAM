Evidence Guide

How to find what you actually need.

A BEAM diagnosis is only as useful as the evidence behind it. These are the practical methods for finding emotional state and decision moment data — before you guess.

Methods

Organized by what each method is best at finding.

  1. Before you start
  2. Analytics
  3. Session recordings
  4. Customer language
  5. Interviews
  6. Surveys
  7. Direct observation
  8. When you have no data

Before you start

You are looking for two things: what the person is feeling at the decision moment, and what conditions are present at that moment. These are different questions that require different methods. Not every method applies to every situation — choose based on what you can realistically access right now.

Evidence does not have to be perfect to be useful. A single session recording of someone abandoning a form tells you more than a week of hypothesizing about why it happens. Start with what you have and flag what you are assuming.

Analytics

Analytics tells you where behavior breaks — not why. Use it to locate the problem before you diagnose it.

Useful for: Emotional state + Decision moment
Drop-off and funnel analysis
Low effort

Find the exact step where people stop. In Google Analytics, look at funnel visualizations or page-level drop-off rates. The page someone leaves from is usually where the barrier exists — ability, environmental, or motivational. High exit rates on a specific field, section, or step are behavioral data.

What to look for: Where does the largest volume of people leave? Is it consistent across devices? Does it happen at a specific field, price reveal, or commitment ask?

Useful for: Decision moment
Time on page and scroll depth
Low effort

Low time on page suggests people are not finding what they expected. High time on page with low conversion suggests they found something — confusion, uncertainty, or a missing piece — that stopped them from acting. Scroll depth shows how far people get before they disengage.

What to look for: Are people reaching the call to action? Are they spending time near the pricing or commitment section and then leaving?

Useful for: Decision moment
Device and timing breakdowns
Low effort

Significant differences in conversion by device often signal an environmental barrier — something that works on desktop but creates friction on mobile. Timing differences can reveal motivational patterns — lower conversion on certain days or times may indicate the offer is not reaching people at the right moment.

Session recordings

Session recordings show you what actually happens at the decision moment. They are one of the most direct evidence sources available for diagnosing both emotional state and environmental barriers.

Useful for: Emotional state + Decision moment
Watch abandonment sessions
Medium effort

Tools like Microsoft Clarity, Hotjar, or FullStory record real user sessions. Filter for sessions where the person reached the key action but did not complete it. Watch what happens in the 30–60 seconds before they leave. Look for hesitation patterns — repeated scrolling, hovering over a field without filling it, clicking back, or reading the same section multiple times.

What to look for: Where does the mouse stop moving? What does the person read more than once? What field or section triggers the exit?

What it tells you about emotional state: Repeated scrolling without action often signals confusion or uncertainty. Clicking back to an earlier page often signals a missing piece of information. Hovering over a commitment field and leaving often signals anxiety or skepticism about what happens next.

Useful for: Decision moment
Heatmaps and click maps
Low effort

Heatmaps show where attention concentrates and where it does not reach. Click maps show what people interact with versus what they ignore. Together they reveal which parts of the decision environment are doing work and which are invisible.

What to look for: Is the primary call to action in a cold zone? Are people clicking on non-clickable elements — a sign they expect something to be there that is not? Is attention concentrated on a section you did not expect?

Customer language

Reviews, support tickets, and cancellation responses contain emotional language people use unprompted. This is your most direct window into emotional state — not what you think they feel, but the words they actually use.

Useful for: Emotional state
Review and feedback mining
Low effort

Read reviews on Google, Yelp, App Store, Trustpilot, or wherever your customers leave them. Read one-star and three-star reviews specifically — they contain friction and emotional language. Look for words that describe a feeling, not just a feature: "I wasn't sure," "I was nervous about," "it wasn't clear," "I felt like."

What to look for: Emotional descriptors. Unmet expectations. Questions the person had that were not answered before they had to decide.

Useful for: Emotional state
Support ticket analysis
Low effort

Support tickets are often questions that people had at the decision moment but could not find answers to. A high volume of questions about a specific topic — pricing, cancellation policy, what happens after signup — tells you what information is missing at the point of commitment and what emotional state that gap creates.

What to look for: Recurring questions. Expressions of hesitation or concern. Language that signals the person wanted to act but needed something first.

Interviews

Interviews give you the most detailed picture of emotional state because you can ask follow-up questions. They are higher effort but often the most revealing method available.

Useful for: Emotional state
Non-converter interviews
High effort

Talk to people who reached the action but did not complete it. Ask: "What stopped you?" and then be quiet. Do not offer options or explanations. Let them describe what happened in their own words. The first answer is usually a rationalization — the real answer usually comes after a follow-up question.

Good follow-up questions: "What were you thinking when you saw that?" / "What would have made you feel more comfortable?" / "Was there a specific moment when you decided not to continue?"

Useful for: Emotional state + Decision moment
Recent converter interviews
High effort

Talk to people who completed the action recently — within the last two weeks if possible, while the memory is fresh. Ask them to walk you through what they were thinking at each step. Ask specifically: "Was there a moment where you almost stopped?" Their answer reveals the emotional state that nearly blocked the action and what resolved it.

Surveys

Surveys are useful when you need to validate a hypothesis at scale or when interviews are not feasible.

Useful for: Emotional state
Exit survey
Medium effort

A single question triggered when someone is about to leave a key page: "What stopped you from completing this today?" with three or four response options and an open field. Keep it to one question — anything more and completion rates drop sharply. Even 20–30 responses can reveal a consistent pattern.

Response options to test: I wasn't sure what would happen next / I needed more information / The timing wasn't right / Something else (open field).

Useful for: Emotional state + Decision moment
Post-conversion survey
Low effort

Ask converters one question immediately after they complete the action: "Was there anything that almost stopped you from completing this?" This surfaces the near-misses — the conditions that were present at the decision moment that almost became barriers. It is one of the fastest ways to find environmental and emotional friction you did not know existed.

Direct observation

For physical environments or any situation where the decision moment happens in person, direct observation is irreplaceable.

Useful for: Emotional state + Decision moment
Walk the experience yourself
Low effort

Go through the experience as if you were the customer. For a physical environment — walk through the entrance, sit in the waiting area, experience the moment before the sales conversation begins. For a digital environment — complete the flow on the device your customer most commonly uses, without any special access or knowledge. Notice what is missing. Notice what creates hesitation.

What to look for: What information do you need that is not available? What does the environment communicate about the offer before anyone says a word? What would make you less comfortable committing?

Useful for: Emotional state + Decision moment
Usability testing
Medium effort

Ask someone unfamiliar with the product or process to complete the action while thinking aloud. Do not help them. Do not explain things. Listen to what confuses them, what they look for that is not there, and what makes them hesitate. Five sessions of unmoderated usability testing will surface more decision-moment evidence than most analytics reports.

When you have no data

Working without evidence

If you are diagnosing a new campaign, a new product, or a situation where no behavioral data exists yet — make your best assumption, label it explicitly as an assumption, and design a test that could prove it wrong. An assumption treated as a fact produces an untestable intervention. An assumption treated as a hypothesis produces a test worth running.

Write it as: "I am assuming the emotional state is [X] because [Y]. This assumption would be disproved if [Z]." Then run the campaign or the intervention and use what you learn to sharpen the next diagnosis.
